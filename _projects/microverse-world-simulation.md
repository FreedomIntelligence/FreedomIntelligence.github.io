<p class="impact-lede">
MicroVerse explores a different kind of world model: not city streets or game worlds, but biological micro-worlds where organ, cellular, and subcellular processes unfold under domain mechanisms that are often invisible to ordinary vision-language models.
</p>

<section class="impact-section">
  <h2>Research Questions</h2>
  <div class="impact-grid">
    <div class="impact-card">
      <strong>Can AI simulate hidden biological dynamics?</strong>
      <p>Micro-world modeling requires reasoning about mechanisms, state transitions, and biological constraints, not only visual appearance.</p>
    </div>
    <div class="impact-card">
      <strong>How should micro-worlds be evaluated?</strong>
      <p>The project builds tasks and benchmarks that test whether generated or predicted dynamics remain biologically meaningful.</p>
    </div>
    <div class="impact-card">
      <strong>What does a scientific world model need?</strong>
      <p>The system points toward simulation models that can be inspected, corrected, and aligned with scientific evidence.</p>
    </div>
  </div>
</section>

<section class="impact-section">
  <h2>Why It Matters</h2>
  <ul class="impact-list">
    <li>It expands world modeling from visually obvious environments to scientific domains where mechanisms are hidden and high-stakes.</li>
    <li>It connects medical AI, biological modeling, and agentic simulation under one research question: can models reason over evolving worlds?</li>
    <li>It complements Economic World Models and TwinMarket by showing that the same simulation agenda can apply to microscopic systems.</li>
  </ul>
</section>
